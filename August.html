1.Hungry Ghost Festival (Various Asian countries):

The Hungry Ghost Festival, also known as the Ghost Month, is a traditional Buddhist and Taoist festival celebrated in many Asian countries, including China, Taiwan, Singapore, Malaysia, and Vietnam. The festival is believed to be a time when the spirits of the deceased return to the world of the living to visit their families and receive offerings.

The history of the Hungry Ghost Festival can be traced back to ancient Buddhist and Taoist beliefs. According to these beliefs, during the seventh month of the lunar calendar (usually August in the Gregorian calendar), the gates of hell are believed to open, allowing the spirits to roam the earth. The festival is a way to honor and appease these spirits, as well as to ensure the well-being of the living.

The Hungry Ghost Festival is marked by various rituals and customs. One of the most common practices is offering food, drink, and other offerings to the spirits, either at home or at temples and shrines. These offerings are believed to provide comfort and sustenance to the spirits and to help them on their journey in the afterlife.

Another important ritual of the Hungry Ghost Festival is the burning of joss paper and other symbolic items. Joss paper, also known as "spirit money," is burned as an offering to the spirits, along with other items such as incense, candles, and paper effigies of material goods. These offerings are believed to bring good fortune and blessings to the spirits and their families.

The Hungry Ghost Festival is also a time for community gatherings and performances, including opera shows, concerts, and other cultural events. These activities are believed to entertain the spirits and to bring joy and happiness to the community.

Overall, the Hungry Ghost Festival is a time to remember and honor deceased loved ones, as well as to reflect on the transient nature of life and the importance of compassion and generosity towards others.

2.International Edinburgh Military Tattoo (Edinburgh, Scotland):

The International Edinburgh Military Tattoo is an annual event held in the grounds of Edinburgh Castle in Scotland. The event is a spectacular showcase of military bands, cultural groups, and performers from around the world, featuring music, dance, and precision marching.

The history of the Edinburgh Military Tattoo dates back to 1950 when the first event was held as part of the Edinburgh International Festival. The original concept was to present a "Tattoo" of military music and performances, similar to the traditional evening performances given by military bands when they were stationed in barracks.

The first Edinburgh Military Tattoo was a small-scale event, featuring only eight items and a single massed pipes and drums performance. However, it was a great success and has since grown into one of the most popular and iconic events in Scotland.

Today, the Edinburgh Military Tattoo attracts audiences of around 220,000 people each year, with millions more watching the televised broadcast of the event. The Tattoo features performances by military bands, drill teams, and cultural groups from around the world, with each year's event having a different theme or focus.

The Edinburgh Military Tattoo is renowned for its grandeur and spectacle, with the backdrop of Edinburgh Castle adding to the atmosphere. The event has become a highlight of the Edinburgh Festival and a must-see for visitors to Scotland, showcasing the best of military and cultural performance from around the world.

3.Taipei Lantern Festival (Taipei, Taiwan):

The Taipei Lantern Festival is an annual event held in Taipei, Taiwan, to mark the end of the Lunar New Year festivities. The festival features beautiful lantern displays, traditional performances, and cultural exhibitions, attracting visitors from Taiwan and around the world.

The history of the Taipei Lantern Festival dates back to the early 20th century, when the Japanese colonial government organized lantern exhibitions in Taipei to celebrate the Lantern Festival, which marks the end of the Lunar New Year celebrations. After World War II, the festival continued to be celebrated, but it wasn't until the 1990s that it evolved into the large-scale event that it is today.

In 1990, the Taipei City Government began organizing the Taipei Lantern Festival as an official event, featuring elaborate lantern displays and cultural performances. The festival quickly became popular among locals and tourists alike, and has since grown into one of the most important festivals in Taiwan.

The Taipei Lantern Festival is known for its creative and artistic lantern displays, which often feature traditional themes and motifs, as well as modern and innovative designs. The festival also includes performances of traditional Taiwanese music, dance, and theater, as well as exhibitions showcasing Taiwanese culture and heritage.

Today, the Taipei Lantern Festival is a major event on Taiwan's cultural calendar, attracting hundreds of thousands of visitors each year. The festival is not only a celebration of the Lantern Festival, but also a showcase of Taiwan's rich cultural heritage and artistic talent.

4. Burning Man (Black Rock Desert, Nevada, USA):

Burning Man is an annual event held in the Black Rock Desert of Nevada, USA, that began in 1986. The event was originally started by Larry Harvey and Jerry James as a small gathering of friends who burned a wooden effigy on the beach in San Francisco. Over the years, Burning Man has grown into one of the largest and most unique festivals in the world, attracting tens of thousands of participants from around the globe.

The ethos of Burning Man is based on principles such as radical self-expression, self-reliance, communal effort, and leaving no trace. Participants, known as "Burners," come together to create a temporary city in the desert, known as Black Rock City, which is dedicated to art, community, and self-expression.

The centerpiece of Burning Man is the burning of a large wooden sculpture, known as "The Man," which takes place on the Saturday night of the event. The burning of The Man symbolizes the climax of the event and is followed by the burning of other sculptures and art installations throughout the week.

Burning Man is also known for its vibrant art installations, theme camps, and mutant vehicles, which are creatively modified vehicles that roam the desert. The event features a wide range of activities, including music performances, art workshops, yoga classes, and more, all organized by participants.

Burning Man has become known for its unique culture and ethos, which encourages participants to express themselves creatively and to engage with the community in meaningful ways. The event has inspired similar events and communities around the world, making it a truly global phenomenon.

5.Obon Festival (Japan):

The Obon Festival, also known as Bon Festival, is a traditional Japanese Buddhist event that honors the spirits of ancestors. The festival is based on the belief that during Obon, the spirits of the deceased return to visit their families. Obon is celebrated with various customs and rituals to welcome and honor the spirits.

The history of the Obon Festival dates back over 500 years to the Muromachi period in Japan (1336-1573). The festival is believed to have originated from the Buddhist legend of Maha Maudgalyayana, a disciple of Buddha, who used his supernatural powers to see his deceased mother suffering in the realm of hungry ghosts. Maha Maudgalyayana sought Buddha's guidance on how to help his mother, and Buddha advised him to make offerings to the monks who had just completed their summer retreat. Maha Maudgalyayana followed Buddha's advice and as a result, his mother was freed from suffering.

In gratitude, Maha Maudgalyayana danced with joy, which is said to be the origin of the Bon Odori dance, a traditional dance performed during Obon. The Bon Odori dance varies by region in Japan and is accompanied by taiko drumming and traditional Japanese music.

Obon is typically observed for three days, although the exact dates vary by region in Japan. The festival includes rituals such as cleaning and decorating ancestral graves, offering food and drink to the spirits, and performing the Bon Odori dance. Lanterns are also lit to guide the spirits back to the other world at the end of the festival.

Today, the Obon Festival is an important cultural event in Japan and is celebrated with a mix of religious observances and community gatherings. It is a time for families to come together to honor their ancestors and to reflect on the importance of family and tradition.
